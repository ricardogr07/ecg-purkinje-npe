## P0. Novelty defense (the existential question)

**The question a judge asks first: has someone already published our exact combination?**

We swept arXiv (2021 to 2026) and PubMed (2020 onward) across the term matrix (simulation-based inference cardiac/ECG; neural posterior estimation ECG/conduction; amortized inference cardiac electrophysiology; Purkinje inverse ECG; identifiability of ventricular activation and conduction velocity; eikonal SBI), and followed PubMed related-articles off the two closest known works. The combination that defines our contribution, **amortized + formally calibrated + per-parameter-quantified identifiability, Purkinje-specific, from the ECG**, does not appear in any work found. The two constituent axes exist, but separately: amortized neural posterior estimation for cardiac problems is established in an adjacent organ system (whole-body hemodynamics, mapping pressure waveforms to physiological parameters: Manduchi et al. 2024, arXiv:2412.17542; Wehenkel et al. 2023, arXiv:2307.13918), while inference of the Purkinje/conduction system from the ECG is established but uncalibrated and non-amortized (Grandits et al. 2024; Alvarez-Barrientos et al. 2025; Camps et al. 2021). No competitor closer than the two already identified (Grandits 2024, Alvarez-Barrientos 2025) surfaced. The discovery that amortized cardiac NPE already exists in hemodynamics does not weaken our claim; it sharpens it, because it means our contribution is precisely the conduction/Purkinje-from-ECG instance with a formal identifiability characterization, not the amortized-NPE method itself.

## P1. Identifiability and non-uniqueness of the ECG inverse problem

###  Identifiability and non-uniqueness of the ECG inverse problem

The reconstruction of cardiac electrical activation from the body-surface ECG is a classically ill-posed inverse problem, and a coherent line of work has now moved this from a general concern to a concrete, demonstrated property of conduction-system personalization. Grandits, Effland, Pock, Krause, Plank, and Pezzuto (GEASI, 2021) framed earliest-activation-site (EAS) identification from the anisotropic eikonal model as a Hamilton-Jacobi optimization, and showed that the approach can not only localize sites but also estimate their number via a topological gradient and be driven directly by an ECG mismatch, which is precisely the "how many early activation sites are enough" question. Camps and colleagues (2021), including Blanca Rodriguez as senior author, built a sequential-Monte-Carlo Approximate Bayesian Computation (SMC-ABC) inference method coupled to eikonal simulations and CMR-derived torso-biventricular models, jointly inferring continuous conduction speeds and discrete earliest activation sites from ECG on a cohort of twenty virtual subjects; the personalization is per-subject and reports accuracy but no formal posterior-calibration diagnostic. Grandits, Gillette, Plank, and Pezzuto (2024) then made the non-uniqueness explicit: they demonstrate, in their words, that distinct activation maps can generate identical surface ECGs, and respond with a physiological Purkinje-muscle-junction (PMJ) prior plus a digital-twin ensemble for probabilistic inference. Taken together, this thread establishes that (i) the ECG-to-activation map is non-injective, and (ii) the field's current answer is a per-subject, optimization-or-ensemble reconstruction with a physiological prior.

Our contribution starts where existence-of-non-uniqueness ends. Rather than re-demonstrating that the map is degenerate, we characterize that degeneracy quantitatively and in an amortized, calibration-audited way at fixed anatomy: per-parameter contraction (posterior_std / prior_std), the degeneracy and correlation structure among conduction parameters, and multimodality, produced by a single amortized neural posterior estimator whose reliability is then formally tested (Theme P1b). This is a measurement of the identifiability structure, not a new inference method and not a rediscovery of the non-uniqueness that Grandits 2024 already reported.

## P1. Calibration in scientific simulation-based inference

###  Calibration in scientific simulation-based inference

The central methodological risk of neural simulation-based inference (SBI) is that a trained posterior can look sharp and be wrong. Hermans, Delaunoy, Rozet, Wehenkel, Begy, and Louppe (2021) provide extensive empirical evidence that the standard SBI estimators (Neural Posterior, Neural Ratio, and Neural Likelihood Estimation, and ABC variants) can yield overconfident, computationally unfaithful posteriors, and argue for conservative estimators and, empirically, for ensembling. This is exactly the failure mode that makes a raw posterior contraction meaningless unless its coverage is tested. Simulation-Based Calibration (Talts, Betancourt, Simpson, Vehtari, Gelman, 2018) is the workflow-level check that the data-averaged posterior matches the prior via rank-statistic uniformity; TARP (Lemos, Coogan, Hezaveh, Perreault-Levasseur, 2023) is a sampling-based coverage test that, unlike earlier coverage methods, needs no posterior density evaluations and is proven necessary and sufficient for posterior accuracy, which suits a normalizing-flow NPE in higher dimensions. Where a diagnostic reveals miscalibration, conformal recalibration offers finite-sample marginal-coverage guarantees: Patel, McNamara, Loper, Regier, and Tewari (2023) give CANVI, a conformalized amortized inference procedure that wraps candidate posterior approximators to produce regions with user-specified coverage in the likelihood-free setting.

The load-bearing point for our positioning is a negative result about the cardiac-conduction SBI literature itself. In targeted searches of arXiv and PubMed/PMC (queries combining simulation-based inference / neural posterior estimation / amortized with cardiac / electrocardiogram and with calibration / coverage), we found no cardiac conduction-personalization SBI paper that reports a formal posterior-calibration diagnostic (SBC, expected coverage, or TARP). The Bayesian cardiac-conduction papers we did verify (Camps 2021 SMC-ABC, Alvarez-Barrientos 2025 BO+ABC, Grandits 2024 ensemble) report accuracy or ensemble spread, not calibration. Formal calibration is therefore the concrete axis on which our amortized NPE differs from the uncalibrated BO+ABC and ensemble line: we do not merely produce a posterior over conduction parameters, we audit whether that posterior's stated uncertainty is trustworthy before drawing identifiability conclusions from it.

## P2. Forward-model fidelity and the eikonal-ECG lead field

###  Forward-model fidelity and the eikonal-ECG lead field

Our forward model (fractal-tree Purkinje into a fast-iterative-method eikonal solver into a 12-lead ECG synthesized with a `1/|r|` infinite-homogeneous-medium kernel at assumed standard electrode positions; no torso volume conductor) sits on an established but imperfect modeling chain, and the literature both justifies our choices and quantifies where a fidelity gap is expected. Eikonal ECG surrogates have been matched directly to clinical 12-lead recordings for patient-specific model building (Grandits Geodesic-BP; Camps digital twinning), which is what makes an eikonal-plus-lead-field forward operator a defensible observation model rather than a toy. The residual mismatch between such surrogates and measured ECGs is not a defect unique to us: Zappon et al. explicitly quantify the variability that persists in state-of-the-art cardiac-digital-twin ECG models even after calibration, which is exactly the kind of quantity our Result B reports. On the conduction-parameter side, Tanikella et al. performed a forward Sobol sensitivity analysis of QRS morphology to His-Purkinje structure and found that most small structural changes move individual QRS features little while specific parameter combinations produce large effects, a forward-sensitivity picture that predicts the weak-versus-strong identifiability structure we recover from the posterior. Finally, our decision to scale a single myocardial conduction speed while holding the anisotropy ratio fixed is grounded in direct tissue measurements: myocardial conduction is orthotropic with three distinct propagation directions fastest along fiber, and the reported fiber-versus-transverse velocity ratio (near 2 to 1, with a slower sheet-normal direction) is stable enough across preparations to treat the ratio as a held structural constant while the overall speed varies (Caldwell 2009; anisotropy of the resulting potential fields characterized classically by Roberts and Scher 1982). *(The specific per-direction velocities 0.67 / 0.30 / 0.17 m/s are reported in the Caldwell 2009 body and tables, not in the abstract verified here; see ledger.)*

## P2. Clinical impact framing

###  Clinical impact framing

Identifiability of conduction parameters matters clinically because interventricular and intraventricular electrical delay is a measurable predictor of who benefits from cardiac resynchronization therapy: pacing at sites of late electrical activation or greater interventricular delay is associated with improved CRT response and reverse remodeling (Gold 2018, PMID 30354310; Gold 2017, PMID 29195547). Correcting the underlying conduction defect is now itself a therapeutic target, with a randomized trial showing that conduction-system (left bundle branch) pacing that recaptures intrinsic left bundle branch block yields greater ejection-fraction improvement than conventional biventricular pacing (LBBP-RESYNC; Wang 2022, PMID 36137670). A forward model whose conduction parameters are provably identifiable from the ECG is therefore a route to non-invasive, per-patient conduction characterization for cardiac digital twins, which is precisely the personalization these therapies require and which our calibrated posterior quantifies rather than assumes.

Annotated citations:

- Gold MR, et al. (2018). PMID 30354310. Interventricular / electrical delay and CRT response. Already verified in the project; cited here as the anchor linking electrical delay to CRT benefit.
- Gold MR, Yu Y, Wold N, Day JD. "The role of interventricular conduction delay to predict clinical response with cardiac resynchronization therapy." Heart Rhythm 2017. DOI 10.1016/j.hrthm.2017.10.016; PMID 29195547. In a multicenter randomized CRT-defibrillator cohort, the unpaced RV-LV interventricular electrical delay was assessed against heart-failure clinical outcomes, extending the electrical-delay-predicts-response evidence from reverse remodeling toward hard clinical endpoints. Supports the claim that conduction timing is a clinically actionable quantity.
- Wang Y, Zhu H, Hou X, Wang Z, ... Gold MR, Fan X, Zou J. "Randomized Trial of Left Bundle Branch vs Biventricular Pacing for Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (LBBP-RESYNC)." J Am Coll Cardiol 2022. DOI 10.1016/j.jacc.2022.07.019; PMID 36137670. Prospective randomized trial in nonischemic cardiomyopathy with LBBB: left bundle branch pacing gave significantly greater LVEF improvement than biventricular pacing (mean difference 5.6%, 95% CI 0.3 to 10.9, P = 0.039). Establishes that correcting the specific conduction defect is therapeutically superior, raising the value of identifying conduction parameters non-invasively.
